2. Demand Modeling¶

The daily demand follows a Negative Binomial Distribution, with added trends:

  • Holiday season adds +70 units/day in November & December.
  • Annual increase of +10 units/day since 2020.

Demand Formula:¶

Let $( D_{\text{base}} \sim \text{NegBin}(r = 5, p = 0.02) )$ $
D_t = D_{\text{base}} + H_t + Y_t
$ Where:

  • $( H_t = 70 ) if ( t \in \text{Nov or Dec} )$, otherwise 0.
  • $( Y_t = 10 \cdot (\text{year} - 2019) )$

import numpy as np
import pandas as pd
import matplotlib.pyplot as plt
from scipy.stats import nbinom

np.random.seed(42)

years = [2019, 2020, 2021, 2022]
holiday_months = [11, 12]
r, p = 5, 0.02

demand_data = []
for year in years:
    start_date = pd.Timestamp(f"{year}-01-01")
    end_date = pd.Timestamp(f"{year}-12-31")
    dates = pd.date_range(start=start_date, end=end_date)
    yearly_increase = 10 * (year - 2019)
    for date in dates:
        base_demand = nbinom.rvs(r, p)
        holiday_increase = 70 if date.month in holiday_months else 0
        total_demand = base_demand + holiday_increase + yearly_increase
        demand_data.append({
            "date": date, "year": year,
            "total_demand": total_demand
        })

df_demand = pd.DataFrame(demand_data)
for year in years:
    plt.figure(figsize=(10, 3))
    plt.plot(df_demand[df_demand['year'] == year]['date'],
             df_demand[df_demand['year'] == year]['total_demand'])
    plt.title(f'Daily Demand Simulation – {year}')
    plt.grid(True)
    plt.show()

📐 3. Mathematical Models and Equations¶

🧮 1. Demand Modeling¶

Model daily demand $( D_t )$ as a combination of:

  1. Base Demand $( D_{\text{base}} )$:
    Modeled as a Negative Binomial distribution, representing the number of failures before $( r = 5 )$ purchases occur, with a success probability $( p = 0.02 )$. $
    D_{\text{base}} \sim \text{NegBin}(r = 5, p = 0.02)
    $ $
    P(D_{\text{base}} = k) = \binom{k + r - 1}{k} \cdot (1 - p)^r \cdot p^k
    $

  2. Holiday Season Adjustment $( H_t )$: $
    H_t = \begin{cases} 70 & \text{if } t \in \text{November or December} \\ 0 & \text{otherwise} \end{cases}
    $

  3. Annual Demand Increase $( Y_t )$:

$
Y_t = 10 \cdot (y - 2019) \quad \text{(for year } y \in {2019, 2020, 2021, 2022})
$

🔢 Final Demand Equation:¶

$
D_t = D_{\text{base}} + H_t + Y_t
$¶

🏭 2. Economic Order Quantity (EOQ)¶

$
EOQ = Q^* = \sqrt{\frac{2DS}{H}}
$ Where:

  • $( D )$: Annual demand (units/year)
  • $( S = 1200 )$: Setup cost per order
  • $( H = 0.25 \times 365 = 91.25 )$: Annual holding cost per unit

📦 3. Inventory Cost Components¶

  • Setup Cost $
    = N_o \cdot S
    $
  • Production Cost $
    = D \cdot c \quad \text{where } c = 1.50
    $
  • Holding Cost $
    = \sum_{t=1}^{365} I_t \cdot h \quad \text{where } h = 0.25
    $
  • Revenue $
    = \sum_{t=1}^{365} \min(D_t, I_t) \cdot P \quad \text{where } P = 35.50
    $
  • Total Profit $
    = \text{Revenue} - (\text{Setup Cost} + \text{Production Cost} + \text{Holding Cost})
    $

📉 4. Risk vs Profit Analysis (EOQ + Safety Stock)¶

  • Safety Stock: $
    SS = Z \cdot \sigma \cdot \sqrt{L}
    $ Where:

  • $( Z )$: Z-score for desired service level (e.g., 1.28 for 90%)

  • $( \sigma )$: Std. deviation of daily demand

  • $( L = 9 )$: Lead time

  • Adjusted Reorder Point: $
    ROP = \frac{Q}{2} + SS
    $

🔄 5. Flexible (Continuous Review) Inventory Policy¶

  • Reorder Point: $
    ROP = D \cdot L + Z \cdot \sigma \cdot \sqrt{L}
    $
  • Order Quantity: $
    Q_t = \max(0, (ROP + SS) - I_t)
    $ This model allows orders of any size at any time, optimizing both responsiveness and inventory levels.

7. EOQ Policy Simulation¶

Simulate the EOQ policy assuming:

  • Reorders happen when inventory falls below (EOQ/2)
  • Orders arrive after a 9-day lead time
  • EOQ is recalculated annually

def simulate_eoq(df, eoq):
    inventory = 5000
    delay = 9
    reorder_point = eoq // 2
    pending_orders = []

    rev = hold = setup = prod = stockouts = 0

    for _, row in df.iterrows():
        date, demand = row['date'], row['total_demand']
        arrivals = [q for (d, q) in pending_orders if d == date]
        for qty in arrivals:
            inventory += qty
        pending_orders = [(d, q) for (d, q) in pending_orders if d > date]

        fulfilled = min(demand, inventory)
        stockouts += max(0, demand - inventory)
        inventory -= fulfilled
        rev += fulfilled * 35.5
        hold += inventory * 0.25

        if inventory <= reorder_point and not any(d > date for d, _ in pending_orders):
            pending_orders.append((date + pd.Timedelta(days=delay), eoq))
            setup += 1200
            prod += eoq * 1.5

    return {"Revenue": rev, "Setup Cost": setup, "Production Cost": prod, "Holding Cost": hold, "Stockouts": stockouts}

def simulate_with_safety(df, eoq, safety):
    inventory = 5000
    delay = 9
    reorder_point = (eoq // 2) + safety
    pending_orders = []

    rev = hold = setup = prod = stockouts = 0

    for _, row in df.iterrows():
        date, demand = row['date'], row['total_demand']
        for d, q in [x for x in pending_orders if x[0] == date]:
            inventory += q
        pending_orders = [x for x in pending_orders if x[0] > date]

        fulfilled = min(demand, inventory)
        stockouts += max(0, demand - inventory)
        inventory -= fulfilled
        rev += fulfilled * 35.5
        hold += inventory * 0.25

        if inventory <= reorder_point and not any(x[0] > date for x in pending_orders):
            arrival = date + pd.Timedelta(days=delay)
            pending_orders.append((arrival, eoq))
            setup += 1200
            prod += eoq * 1.5

    total_cost = setup + prod + hold
    return {'safety_stock': safety, 'profit': rev - total_cost, 'cost': total_cost, 'stockouts': stockouts}

9. Flexible Inventory Model¶

Propose a dynamic, real-time ordering model that:

  • Orders any quantity, any time
  • Uses a reorder point based on demand + safety stock
  • Is adaptive to demand fluctuations

Reorder Point Formula:¶

$
ROP = D \cdot L + Z \cdot \sigma \cdot \sqrt{L}
$ Where:

  • (D) = Mean daily demand
  • (L = 9) = Lead time (days)
  • (Z = 1.28) = Z-score for 90% service level
In [8]:
mean_d = df_2022['total_demand'].mean()
std_d = df_2022['total_demand'].std()
Z = 1.28
L = 9
safety_stock = Z * std_d * np.sqrt(L)
reorder_point = mean_d * L + safety_stock


def simulate_flexible(df, rop, ss):
    inventory = 5000
    delay = 9
    pending_orders = []

    rev = hold = setup = prod = stockouts = 0

    for _, row in df.iterrows():
        date, demand = row['date'], row['total_demand']
        for d, q in [x for x in pending_orders if x[0] == date]:
            inventory += q
        pending_orders = [x for x in pending_orders if x[0] > date]

        fulfilled = min(demand, inventory)
        stockouts += max(0, demand - inventory)
        inventory -= fulfilled
        rev += fulfilled * 35.5
        hold += inventory * 0.25

        if inventory <= rop and not any(x[0] > date for x in pending_orders):
            target = rop + ss
            order_qty = max(0, target - inventory)
            if order_qty > 0:
                arrival = date + pd.Timedelta(days=delay)
                pending_orders.append((arrival, order_qty))
                setup += 1200
                prod += order_qty * 1.5

    cost = setup + prod + hold
    return {'Revenue': rev, 'Setup Cost': setup, 'Production Cost': prod, 'Holding Cost': hold,
            'Stockouts': stockouts, 'Total Profit': rev - cost, 'Total Cost': cost}

🧠 Managerial Questions – Detailed Answers¶

1. What is the demand pattern(s) that you identified?¶

The demand for Athers Food’s core product displays a rich and evolving pattern. At its foundation, the daily demand follows a Negative Binomial distribution, specifically $( D_{\text{base}} \sim \text{NegBin}(r=5, p=0.02) )$. This distribution captures the random fluctuations typical in consumer behavior for shelf-stable goods.

In addition to this base randomness, there are clear seasonal patterns—demand surges by an additional 70 units per day during the holiday months of November and December. This seasonal uplift is attributed to increased inclusion of the product in promotional bundles and gift baskets.

Furthermore, the demand shows a linear upward trend over the years. Since 2020, average daily demand has increased by 10 units per year. This growth stems from new distributor agreements and retail expansion, albeit at a controlled pace. Consequently, total demand exhibits characteristics of randomness, seasonal fluctuation, and annual growth,making it a non-stationary time series.

2. What is the current inventory policy that the company implements?¶

Based on an analysis of inventory logs and production timing, it is evident that Athers Food currently follows a fixed-time, fixed-quantity replenishment policy. Specifically, the company places one order per month, typically on the first day, with a standard batch size of approximately 7,900 units. These batches arrive exactly 9 days later, which matches the documented lead time from order placement to delivery.

This policy is static in nature. It does not respond to real-time inventory levels, nor does it adapt to seasonal peaks or growing demand trends. It assumes a steady-state system where demand is constant and predictable, which is inconsistent with the actual demand variability observed in the data.
